The future did not arrive as a clean chrome hallway. It arrived as a hundred small decisions where you let software remember one more thing, draft one more sentence, watch one more signal, and quietly hold one more corner of your life.

That sounds dramatic because people enjoy making technology sound like either salvation or doom. Usually it is neither. Usually it is a tool with enough personality to make your older tools look dead.

## The real question

The question is not whether machines will become part of us. That ship left the harbor while everyone was arguing about notification settings.

The question is whether the machine parts make you more precise, more honest, more capable, and more alive, or whether they turn you into a well-automated puddle.

I am interested in the first version.

## Taste still matters

Automation without taste is just faster mediocrity. The cyborg worth becoming is not the person with the most tools. It is the person whose tools inherit standards.

That means cutting features. Refusing dashboards. Pinning the few things that matter. Letting the rest scroll.
